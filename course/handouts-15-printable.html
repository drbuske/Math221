    Section Overview

    Functions are a powerful tool in mathematics: each describes a rule or process that takes any valid input to one and only one output. One of the natural questions that arises regarding any function is ‚Äúcan the rule be undone (or reversed)?‚Äù Indeed, this is connected to the concept of a function‚Äôs inverse. While not every function has an inverse, for those that do, knowing the inverse can be valuable in a wide range of settings.
    üîó
    Among the most important inverse functions in mathematics are the natural logarithm function, \(\ln(x)\) (which is the inverse of the exponential function, \(e^{x}\)) and the inverse trigonometric functions, such as \(\arcsin(x)\) and \(\arctan(x)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    In this section of the text, we explore how we can use the relationship between a function and its inverse to determine the derivative formula for the inverse function and, along the way, learn a key geometric connection between a function‚Äôs derivative and the derivative of its inverse.
    üîó
    üîó
